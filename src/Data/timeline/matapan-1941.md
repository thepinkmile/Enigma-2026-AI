## The Intelligence

In late March 1941, Bletchley Park decrypted Italian Navy signals encrypted on the **Enigma C38m** (an Italian-adapted Enigma variant) revealing that a major Italian force — including the battleship
*Vittorio Veneto* and three heavy cruisers — planned to attack British troop convoys south of Crete.

Admiral Cunningham, commanding the British Mediterranean Fleet at Alexandria, received the intelligence with enough lead time to intercept. To protect the source, he staged a shore-leave exercise to
mislead Japanese diplomats (who might inform the Axis) before quietly putting to sea.

## The Battle

On **28 March 1941**:

- British forces made contact with the Italian fleet south of Greece
- *Vittorio Veneto* was damaged by torpedo aircraft from HMS *Formidable*
- The heavy cruiser *Pola* was crippled and stopped dead in the water
- The Italian command sent two more heavy cruisers (*Zara* and *Fiume*) with four destroyers to assist *Pola* — unaware the British had radar and were nearby
- Admiral Cunningham's battleships *Warspite*, *Valiant*, and *Barham* engaged at close range in darkness, using radar

**Italian losses:**

- 3 heavy cruisers: *Zara*, *Fiume*, *Pola*
- 2 destroyers: *Alfieri*, *Carducci*
- ~2,300 men killed
- 1 heavy cruiser *Vittorio Veneto* damaged

**British losses:** None

## Significance

Matapan demonstrated several key principles that would govern Ultra intelligence use for the rest of the war:

1. **Intelligence had to be used** — not hoarded for fear of revealing the source
2. **Cover stories were essential** — the shore-leave deception was one of the first deliberate source-protection measures
3. **Timing was critical** — the gap between intercept, decryption, and action had to be short enough to be militarily useful

The victory also confirmed to senior British commanders that signals intelligence was worth extraordinary effort and security. Cunningham's report to the Admiralty noted the "most timely and accurate
intelligence" without revealing its source.
