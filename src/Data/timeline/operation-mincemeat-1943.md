## The Plan

Operation Mincemeat was conceived by Flight Lt. Charles Cholmondeley (MI5) and developed by Commander Ewen Montagu (Naval Intelligence). The plan:

1. Obtain a recently deceased human body (Glyndwr Michael, a Welsh vagrant who had died of rat poison)
2. Dress the body as **Major William Martin, Royal Marines** with plausible personal effects (love letters, theatre ticket stubs, an overdraft notice)
3. Prepare false documents to be carried in a locked briefcase chained to the body — specifically, a personal letter from General Sir Archibald Nye (VCIGS) to General Alexander, appearing to reveal
  that the planned Allied invasion of Southern Europe would target **Greece and Sardinia** — not Sicily
4. Plant the body off the Spanish coast to drift ashore where Spanish (and German) intelligence was active
5. Spanish authorities passed the documents to the Abwehr

## Confirmation via Enigma

The critical evidence that Mincemeat had worked came from **Abwehr Enigma decrypts**. Dilly Knox's team (now led by others after Knox's death in February 1943) read the Abwehr's communications
confirming that the Germans had received and believed the documents.

The decrypt of the Abwehr assessment read: *"The genuineness of the documents is beyond doubt."*

Hitler personally ordered reinforcements to **Sardinia and Greece** and transferred Panzer units from Sicily. When the real Allied invasion of **Sicily (Operation Husky)** began on 10 July 1943, Axis
defences were significantly weakened.

## Significance

Mincemeat demonstrates the mature operation of the Double Cross / Ultra system: Enigma decrypts not only provided intelligence but **confirmed that strategic deceptions had succeeded**. The ability
to read the enemy's assessment of your own deception is intelligence at its most powerful — it allows the deception to be calibrated and verified in real time.

Casualty estimates for the Sicily campaign suggest the German redeployment saved several thousand Allied lives.

## Declassification and Legacy

Operation Mincemeat remained classified until Ewen Montagu published **The Man Who Never Was** in 1953 — one of the first wartime intelligence accounts to appear publicly (predating the Ultra
revelations by 21 years). However, Montagu could not reveal the Enigma dimension; that remained secret until 1974. The full story was told by Ben Macintyre in **Operation Mincemeat** (2010).
