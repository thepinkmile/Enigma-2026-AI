## The Context

By July 1939, war with Germany was clearly imminent. The Polish Cipher Bureau had been reading German Enigma traffic since December 1932 — a secret they had protected for seven years. In September
1938, Germany had added **two more rotors** to the available set (from 3 to 5, requiring operators to choose 3 from 5) and increased the plugboard connections from 6 to 10. The Poles' existing
methods were overwhelmed.

Poland could not defeat this alone. The decision was taken at the highest levels of Polish intelligence to share everything with the Allied services before the war began.

## The Meeting

On **25 July 1939**, a conference was held at a secret signals intelligence station in the **Kabaty Woods**, south of Warsaw at a location called **Pyry**. The Polish team presented:

1. **The complete wiring of all Enigma rotors** (I through VIII by this date) — the result of Rejewski's 1932 mathematical reconstruction
2. **Rejewski's mathematical methods** — the permutation-group approach to breaking the double indicator
3. **Two fully operational replica Enigma machines** — built by the Poles and handed to the British and French delegations respectively
4. **Zygalski sheets** (*perforated sheets*) — Henryk Zygalski's punched-card method for finding rotor positions
5. **Bomba kryptologiczna designs** — the Polish electromechanical bombe

## British Reaction

Alastair Denniston later wrote that the meeting was "the most important cryptographic conference of the war." Dilly Knox — who had been attempting to reconstruct the Enigma's wiring independently for
years — received the correct wiring from the Poles. His reported first words were: *"We never thought of that!"* (referring to the reflector's role in the construction).

## The Transfer

By early August 1939, each delegation had received:

- One replica Enigma machine
- Copies of the Zygalski sheets
- Documentation of the mathematical methods

The British machine and materials arrived at **Bletchley Park**, which was being prepared as the wartime signals intelligence centre. It took Bletchley until **January 1940** to break the first
wartime Enigma message — building on the Polish foundation.

## Polish Fate

Six weeks after the Pyry conference, Germany invaded Poland (1 September 1939). The Cipher Bureau staff evacuated south and east, eventually reaching **France** via Romania. They continued breaking
Enigma in France under Bertrand's direction at Château des Fouzes. After France fell in 1940, they operated from Vichy France until 1942, then were captured by the Germans in 1943. Różycki had
drowned in a Mediterranean ship sinking in January 1942.

## Legacy

The Pyry conference is the critical junction between the Polish work of 1932–1939 and the British work of 1939–1945. Without the Polish handover, Bletchley Park would have needed years to reach the
same starting point. Historians estimate the handover brought forward the first Enigma break at BP by at least 12–18 months.
