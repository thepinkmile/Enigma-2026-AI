## The Patent

On **23 February 1918**, electrical engineer Arthur Scherbius filed German patent **DE416219** for an electrically operated cipher machine based on rotating wired wheels. The patent described a
machine that:

- Used electrical contacts to wire 26 input positions (one per letter) to 26 output positions in a scrambled permutation
- Stacked multiple such **rotors** in series so that the signal passed through successive permutations
- **Advanced one or more rotors** after each keypress, changing the permutation for every letter
- Used a **reflector** (Umkehrwalze) at one end so that the signal reversed direction after passing through all rotors, passed back through them in the opposite direction, and lit a lamp

This design produced **reciprocal encryption** (enciphering and deciphering used the same machine settings — the same keypress that encrypted `A` to `Z` would also encrypt `Z` to `A`), a critical
operational advantage.

## Why the Date Matters

The patent filing date of 23 February 1918 is the **zero point** of the Enigma's history. Hugo Koch filed a similar independent patent in the Netherlands in October 1919 — 20 months later. The
commercial company ChiMaAG was not founded until 9 July 1923. The German military did not adopt Enigma until 1926. Everything traces back to this one patent.

## The Machine Described

The initial patent described a machine with:

- **4 rotors** (later commercial versions used 3; military versions used 3 with an optional 4th)
- **Typewriter-style keyboard** input
- **Lamp board** output showing the ciphertext letter
- Battery power
- A rotor stepping mechanism engaged by the keypress

The commercial Enigma A (1923) was heavier and more cumbersome than the description, featuring a typewriter-printout attachment. Subsequent versions (Enigma B, C, D, I) progressively refined the
design toward the military standard.
