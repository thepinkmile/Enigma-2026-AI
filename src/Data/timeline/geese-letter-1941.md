## The Crisis

By October 1941, Bletchley Park was severely under-resourced. The Bombe programme was breaking increasing volumes of Enigma traffic, but:

- **Hut 6** (Army/Air Force Enigma decryption) was short of typists and registration staff
- **Hut 8** (Naval Enigma) urgently needed mathematically-trained codebreakers for Banburismus
- **The Bombe programme** needed more machines and operators
- Administrative bottlenecks — requiring staff requests to go through normal civil service channels — were delaying urgent hires by months

Alastair Denniston had attempted to resolve this through proper channels and failed.

## The Letter

On 21 October 1941 (Trafalgar Day — a deliberate choice), the four men wrote directly to Winston Churchill, bypassing Denniston and all intermediate authority. Stuart Milner-Barry personally
delivered the letter to Downing Street, since none of the four had authority to contact the Prime Minister directly.

The letter stated plainly that they were not producing as much intelligence as they could because of staffing shortages, and listed specific, concrete requirements.

Churchill's response on the **same day** — 21 October 1941 — was a handwritten note to his Chief of Staff, General Hastings Ismay:

> *"Make sure they have all they want on extreme priority and report to me that this has been done."*

He also wrote on the note: **"ACTION THIS DAY"** — his shorthand for immediate compliance.

## Consequences

Within weeks:

- Additional staff were approved for all named sections
- The Bombe programme was authorised for expanded capacity
- Administrative procedures were streamlined to allow faster hiring

The letter is credited with directly enabling the breakthrough period of late 1941–1942 in which Bletchley's output became operationally decisive.

## The "Geese" Name

The letter is sometimes called the "Geese Letter" because Churchill, upon reading a similar complaint from another department, reportedly asked, "Why am I not to *wring* the necks of the geese that
lay the golden eggs?" The phrase was taken to apply to this episode — the four signatories being the golden geese.
