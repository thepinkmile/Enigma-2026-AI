## The Company

**Chiffriermaschinen Aktiengesellschaft** (ChiMaAG — "Cipher Machines Public Limited Company") was incorporated in Berlin on **9 July 1923**. Its purpose was to commercialise the Enigma machine
  patents held by Scherbius and to manufacture machines for sale to governments and businesses.

### Key Personnel

- **Arthur Scherbius** — inventor, technical director
- **Richard Ritter** — business partner and co-founder
- Hugo Koch's Dutch patents had been purchased by Scherbius and incorporated

### Products

ChiMaAG produced the early commercial Enigma variants:

- **Enigma A** (1923–1925): large, heavy, included a typewriter-style printout; commercially unsuccessful
- **Enigma B** (1924–1925): minor improvements
- **Enigma C** (*Funkschlüssel C*, 1926): first military adoption (German Navy); lighter and more practical
- **Enigma D** (1927): the first commercially successful model; QWERTZ keyboard; ancestor of all military variants

### Scherbius's Death

Arthur Scherbius died on **13 May 1929** — killed in a horse-carriage accident before the Enigma achieved its eventual military prominence. He never knew that his machine would become one of the most
consequential inventions of the 20th century.

### Renaming

After Scherbius's death, the company was restructured. In **1934**, ChiMaAG was renamed **Heimsoeth und Rinke** (H&R) and continued to supply Enigma machines to the German military throughout the war.

## First Public Demonstration

The Enigma was first publicly demonstrated at the **International Postal Union Congress** in **Bern, Switzerland, in 1923** — the same year ChiMaAG was founded. Initial commercial interest was
limited; banks and governments were not yet convinced of the need for machine encryption. Military adoption came later as WWI communications security failures were analysed.
