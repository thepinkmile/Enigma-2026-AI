## The Petition

Computer scientist **John Graham-Cumming** organised an online petition in 2009 demanding a formal government apology for Turing's treatment. The petition gathered approximately **30,000 signatures**
and attracted significant media attention on the eve of the campaign.

## The Apology

Prime Minister **Gordon Brown** released a written statement on 10 September 2009:

> *"While Turing was dealt with under the law of the time and we can't put the clock back, his treatment was of course utterly unfair and I am pleased to have the chance to say how deeply sorry I and
we all are for what happened to him. Alan and the many thousands of other gay men who were convicted as he was convicted under homophobic laws were treated terribly. Over time I hope that we can make
amends for the way he and so many others were treated."*

The statement described Turing as "one of the greatest of the great men" and acknowledged his contribution to winning the Second World War.

## Limitations of the Apology

The 2009 apology was a personal statement by the Prime Minister — not a formal pardon or legal act. Turing's conviction remained on the legal record. Furthermore, the apology was to Turing alone, not
to the thousands of other men convicted under the same laws.

These limitations drove further campaigning that led to the Royal Pardon in 2013 and the Alan Turing Law in 2017.

## Public Impact

The apology was widely reported internationally and significantly raised public awareness of both Turing's war contribution and the injustice of his persecution. It marked the beginning of a period
of public rehabilitation that would ultimately result in:

- Turing's name on the £50 note
- A formal pardon
- The law extending posthumous pardons to all similarly convicted men
