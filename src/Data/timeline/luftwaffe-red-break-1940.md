## Background: The Red Network

The Luftwaffe's main communications Enigma network was assigned the codename **"Red"** by GC&CS (Government Code and Cypher School). It was the highest-volume and most accessible German
military Enigma traffic being intercepted by the British — the Air Force communicated extensively and its operators were considered less disciplined than their Army and Navy counterparts.

Before May 1940, GC&CS could read Red only sporadically. The Germans changed their key-setting procedures on **10 May 1940** — the first day of *Fall Gelb* (Case Yellow), the German invasion
of France and the Low Countries. Paradoxically, this change that was intended to tighten security contained a new operator shortcut that would prove fatal.

## The Herivel Tip

**John Herivel** was a young Cambridge mathematician assigned to Hut 6 in February 1940. Late one evening, he imagined himself as a tired, cold, overworked Enigma operator who had just received
his daily key sheet and been told to set his machine for the new day. Such an operator might:

1. Set the rotors to the day's *Grundstellung* (starting position) as required.
2. Choose his personal three-letter message indicator — and, being lazy, choose the letters his rotors were already showing, rather than spinning them to a random new position.

If many operators did this, the message indicators for the day would **cluster around the Grundstellung**. By looking at the population of indicators received in the first few hours of a new
key period, Hut 6 could infer the approximate Grundstellung — dramatically reducing the search space for the Bombe.

Herivel proposed this idea in February 1940 but was initially sceptical it would work in practice. The key-procedure change of **10 May 1940** created exactly the conditions for the tip to
work — and it did. **David Rees** is credited with making the first confirmed decode using a Herivel-tip shortcut.

## Regular Reading from 22 May 1940

By **22 May 1940** — twelve days into the Battle of France — Hut 6 achieved **daily, reliable reading** of the Red network. The intelligence was passed to RAF Fighter Command and contributed
to situational awareness throughout the **Battle of Britain** (July–October 1940). It was also valuable for understanding Luftwaffe order of battle, target selection, and logistics.

## The Bombe and Diagonal Board

The Herivel tip reduced the search space enough for the existing crib-based Bombe method to succeed within operational time constraints. The first Bombe, *Victory*, had been delivered on
**18 March 1940**. The addition of Gordon Welchman's **Diagonal Board** to the second Bombe (*Agnus Dei*) on **8 August 1940** reduced false stops dramatically, making the operation both
faster and more reliable.

### Naming Note: "Red" vs "Yellow"

The Luftwaffe Enigma network was codenamed **"Red"** — not "Yellow". The German operation to invade France and the Low Countries was codenamed *Fall Gelb* (Case Yellow), which is sometimes
confused with the British codeword for the Enigma network. The codename Red referred specifically to the Luftwaffe cipher network and was used throughout the war.

## Intelligence Value

Regular reading of Red traffic provided:

- **Real-time Luftwaffe operations orders** — giving Fighter Command advance warning of raid targets, strengths, and timings during the Battle of Britain
- **Luftwaffe order of battle** — unit identification, strength, and location data
- **Logistical intelligence** — fuel, ammunition, and serviceable aircraft figures
- **Strategic context** — the Red traffic revealed details of the Luftwaffe's strategic debate between targeting RAF airfields
  vs. switching to city bombing (the "Blitz"), providing crucial warning of the strategic shift in early September 1940

## Significance

The continuous reading of Red from May 1940 was arguably the most operationally important single Enigma break of the war. It predated the naval Enigma breaks and provided a continuous
intelligence stream through the critical period of 1940–1941. It validated the Bombe programme as operationally viable and provided the production justification for the large-scale Bombe
expansion that followed.
