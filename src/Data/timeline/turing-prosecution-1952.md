## The Circumstances

In December 1951, Alan Turing reported a burglary to the Wilmslow police. In the course of the investigation, Turing disclosed that he had been in a relationship with 19-year-old Arnold Murray, and
that Murray had let the burglar (an acquaintance) into Turing's home. Turing was apparently unaware that what he was describing was a criminal offence.

Under the **Criminal Law Amendment Act 1885** (Section 11 — the same law used to prosecute Oscar Wilde in 1895), "gross indecency between male persons" was a criminal offence punishable by up to two
years' hard labour. Both Turing and Murray were charged.

## The Trial

Turing pleaded **guilty** to the charges. At sentencing on 31 March 1952, he was given the choice between imprisonment and **probation with hormonal treatment** (chemical castration via synthetic
oestrogen injections, intended to eliminate sexual drive).

Turing chose probation with treatment. The treatment continued for a year, causing significant physical changes (gynaecomastia) and reportedly severe psychological effects.

## Death

On **7 June 1954**, Turing was found dead at his home in Wilmslow. He was 41. The cause of death was **cyanide poisoning**. An inquest returned a verdict of suicide — he had apparently eaten a
cyanide-laced apple. Some biographers have questioned this verdict, suggesting it may have been accidental (he had cyanide solution in his home for chemistry experiments), but the suicide verdict was
widely accepted.

He had been stripped of his security clearance and barred from any further government work following his conviction.

## The Injustice

Turing's persecution was the culmination of Britain's treatment of gay men in the post-war era — an era when the wartime contribution of many gay servicemen and intelligence staff was simultaneously
being celebrated (covertly) and their private lives being criminalised (overtly). The cruel irony that the man most responsible for breaking the codes that won the war was destroyed by a law his own
country enforced against him was not publicly acknowledged for decades.

The first public recognition came only after the Ultra programme was declassified in 1974 — at which point the connection between Turing's work and the Allied victory became apparent.
