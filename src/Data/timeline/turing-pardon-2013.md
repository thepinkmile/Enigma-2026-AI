## The Pardon

On **24 December 2013**, Queen Elizabeth II granted a **posthumous Royal Pardon** to Alan Turing under the Royal Prerogative of Mercy. The pardon was recommended by Lord Chancellor **Chris Grayling**.

The Government's statement noted that Turing had been "convicted of 'gross indecency' — in effect, for being gay" and that the pardon acknowledged "that the sentence handed down to him was unjust."

## Parliamentary Background

The path to the pardon involved multiple parliamentary attempts:

- **2012**: Lord Sharkey introduced a Private Member's Bill in the House of Lords to grant Turing a statutory pardon; it passed the Lords but was not taken up in the Commons
- **2013**: The Government agreed to act via Royal Prerogative rather than statute
- **24 December 2013**: The pardon was granted

## Criticism

The Royal Pardon was widely celebrated but also drew significant criticism:

- It was **selective** — Turing alone received the pardon, not the estimated **49,000 other men** convicted under the same laws (many still living)
- A pardon implies a criminal conviction was *understandable given the laws of the time* — human rights advocates argued that Turing should be **exonerated** (recognised as never having committed any
  wrongdoing), not merely pardoned
- The selective application appeared to reflect Turing's fame rather than the principle that *all* unjust convictions deserved remedy

These criticisms drove further legislation — the **Alan Turing Law** of 2017, which provided posthumous pardons and legal disregards for all similarly convicted men.

## Context

By December 2013, Turing's public profile was enormous. His role at Bletchley Park had been fully documented; the **Turing Award** (the computing equivalent of the Nobel Prize) had been awarded since
1966; his theoretical work had been integrated into computer science curricula worldwide; the film *The Imitation Game* (released 2014) would reach a global audience. The pardon was, in part,
recognition of this cultural and historical reality.
