## 30 Years of Silence

From 1945 to 1974 — **thirty years** — the Ultra programme, Bletchley Park, the Enigma breaks, the Bombes, and all the intelligence they produced were kept completely secret. The reason was
strategic: Britain and its allies were still using many of the same cipher techniques and wanted adversaries to believe Enigma-class machines were secure. Many nations that had acquired Enigma
machines after the war were using them in the belief that they provided adequate security — nations Britain wished to monitor.

Every Bletchley Park veteran — over 10,000 people — had signed the Official Secrets Act. The overwhelming majority kept the secret faithfully. Husbands did not tell wives. Parents did not tell
children. Historians writing WWII accounts attributed Allied successes to luck, better generals, or superior resources — the intelligence dimension was entirely absent.

## Winterbotham's Book

**Group Captain Frederick William Winterbotham** was the RAF intelligence officer responsible for distributing Ultra intelligence to Allied commanders in the field during the war. In 1974, the
  British government authorised him to publish.

*The Ultra Secret* (1974, Weidenfeld & Nicolson) was a bombshell. It revealed:

- That Germany's Enigma machine had been broken from early in the war
- That "Ultra" was the codename for intelligence derived from high-level enemy cipher traffic
- That Ultra had influenced virtually every major campaign of the war
- That the secret had been kept for 30 years

## The Historical Reassessment

The book triggered an immediate and massive reassessment of WWII history:

- Battles previously attributed to luck or poor German generalship were reanalysed in light of the intelligence
- The North Africa campaign, the Battle of the Atlantic, D-Day — all were re-examined
- Harry Hinsley began work on the official multi-volume *British Intelligence in the Second World War* (1979–1990), which documented the programme comprehensively

## Turing's Rehabilitation — Posthumously

The publication of Ultra also brought belated recognition to Alan Turing — who had been prosecuted in 1952, driven to suicide in 1954, and was entirely unknown to the public. Suddenly, the man most
responsible for breaking Enigma at Bletchley Park became a figure of enormous historical importance — and the injustice of his persecution became visible for the first time.

The path from this publication to the 2009 apology, 2013 pardon, and 2017 law began here.
