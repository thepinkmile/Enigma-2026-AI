## The Action

On 9 May 1941, HMS *Bulldog* (escort commander) and other vessels of Convoy OB-318 were attacked by **U-110** commanded by Fritz-Julius Lemp — the same officer who had controversially sunk the liner
*SS Athenia* on 3 September 1939. After depth-charge attack, U-110 surfaced and was abandoned by most of her crew.

**Lt. David Balme** of HMS *Bulldog* led a boarding party. They found the submarine abandoned but **not scuttled** — Lemp had ordered the crew to abandon ship but failed to ensure the submarine would
  sink before the British arrived. Lemp himself is believed to have been killed attempting to return to the submarine when he realised the capture was happening.

## The Haul

The boarding party systematically searched U-110 and recovered:

- **Enigma machine with rotors in the current daily position** — not reset since the morning key change
- **Current Naval Enigma key settings** for the day
- **Short Signal (*Kurzsignal*) codebook** — the book used to create ultra-compressed weather and sighting reports
- **Weather Short Signal codebook**
- **Bigram tables** (for encrypting U-boat grid references)

These materials were rushed to Bletchley Park. Hut 8 was able to read current and back-traffic. The Naval Enigma (*Heimisch*/*Dolphin* key) was read nearly continuously until February 1942 when the
M4 four-rotor machine was introduced.

## Secrecy of the Capture

The capture was kept secret not only from the public but from U-110's own surviving crew, who were told their submarine had sunk. The crew were held in Canada in isolation for the rest of the war to
prevent them from alerting the Germans.

The official cover story was maintained for 30 years. The British government's acknowledgment only came in 1966; full details remained classified until well after the Ultra secret was declassified in
1974.

## Significance

The U-110 capture is often described as the single most important intelligence capture of the war. By providing current key settings and codebooks, it gave Bletchley Park immediate access to all
Atlantic traffic — enabling the tracking and routing-around of U-boat wolf packs at the height of the Battle of the Atlantic. Bletchley analysts calculated this saved hundreds of thousands of tons of
Allied shipping and shortened the war by months.
