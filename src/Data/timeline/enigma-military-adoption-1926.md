## The Decision

In 1926, the German Navy (*Reichsmarine*) became the **first military organisation in the world** to adopt a rotor-based cipher machine for operational communications. The machine adopted was the
**Enigma C** (*Funkschlüssel C* — "Radio Cipher C"), a compact, lightweight variant designed specifically for naval use.

The Navy's adoption reflected lessons learned from WWI, in which British naval intelligence (Room 40) had read German fleet communications — with catastrophic consequences at the Battle of Jutland
and in the campaign against the U-boats. The Enigma's complexity appeared to offer a definitive solution to radio interception.

## The Machine

The Enigma C featured:

- **3 rotors** (wheels) chosen from a set
- **Reflector** (Umkehrwalze) — Willi Korn's invention
- No plugboard (*Steckerbrett*) — added later with the Wehrmacht Enigma I
- Compact case designed for naval vessels

## Subsequent Adoptions

- **1928**: German Army (*Reichswehr*) adopts the Enigma I with plugboard
- **1930**: Enigma I issued to Army signals units
- **1935**: Luftwaffe adopts the same Enigma I standard
- **1939**: War begins with all three services using the same basic machine, but different key settings and procedures

## The Plugboard Question

The naval Enigma C **did not have a plugboard**. The Wehrmacht Enigma I, adopted in 1928 (before the Navy moved to the M1/M2/M3 series), **did have a plugboard** — adding an additional substitution
step that dramatically increased the number of possible key settings. By the time the Polish Cipher Bureau began attacking Enigma in 1932, they were facing the plugboard version, which required
different attacks from the non-plugboard naval models.
