## The Action

On **30 October 1942**, HMS *Petard* (destroyer) and other ships of the 12th Destroyer Flotilla located and attacked **U-559** (Kapitänleutnant Hans Heidtmann) in the Eastern Mediterranean using
depth charges over nine hours.

As U-559 surfaced and her crew abandoned ship, three men from *Petard* dived into the dark Mediterranean and boarded the sinking submarine:

- **Lt. Francis Fasson** (27) — first officer
- **Able Seaman Colin Grazier** (22) — sailor
- **Tommy Brown** (16) — NAAFI canteen assistant (a civilian, technically, who had falsified his age to serve)

In the flooded control room, working in near-darkness as the submarine took on water, Fasson and Grazier passed documents up through the conning tower hatch to Tommy Brown, who was holding on
outside. Brown was able to swim to a waiting whaler and pass the documents to the surface before the submarine sank suddenly.

Fasson and Grazier drowned when U-559 went down. Tommy Brown survived.

## The Documents

The recovered materials included:

- The **Short Weather Cipher book** (*Wetterkurzschlüssel*) — used to transmit weather reports in compressed form
- The **Short Signal book** (*Kurzsignalheft*) — used for U-boat contact and sighting reports

These were not the M4 Enigma codebooks directly — but they provided something equally valuable: the books showed how weather signals sent in **four-rotor Shark** were simultaneously broadcast in the
older **three-rotor Dolphin** key. This gave Hut 8 the **cribs** needed to attack Shark: known plaintext paired with Shark ciphertext.

## The Break

Within weeks of receiving the material, Hut 8 used the cribs with newly delivered four-rotor Bombes to break the M4 Shark settings for the first time. By **December 1942**, the Shark blackout was
over. Atlantic convoy routing intelligence was restored.

## Honours

- **Francis Fasson**: George Cross (posthumous)
- **Colin Grazier**: George Cross (posthumous)
- **Tommy Brown**: George Medal

The George Cross — Britain's highest civilian gallantry award (and the highest military gallantry award below the Victoria Cross) — was awarded posthumously to both Fasson and Grazier. Fasson and
Grazier are buried at sea. Grazier is commemorated by a memorial in his home town of Tamworth, Staffordshire. Fasson is commemorated at the Commonwealth War Graves Commission.
