## The Abwehr Enigma

The **Abwehr** (German military intelligence, *Amt Ausland/Abwehr* — "Foreign/Defence Office") used a modified Enigma variant called the **Enigma G** (also known as the *Abwehr Enigma*). The Abwehr
Enigma differed from the Wehrmacht Enigma in crucial ways:

- **No plugboard** (*Steckerbrett*) — considered less secure than military Enigma
- **Different stepping mechanism** — the rotors stepped at irregular intervals (the *Abwehr* machine used a gear counter mechanism rather than the standard notch)
- **Different rotor wirings** from the Wehrmacht set
- The machine was used by Abwehr agents in Spain, Portugal, and other neutral countries to communicate with Berlin

## Knox's Team

Dilly Knox's section, working in **The Cottage** — a small cottage attached to the main Bletchley Park mansion — had been working on the Abwehr Enigma since the outbreak of war. Knox was by this time
very ill with lymphoma (he died in February 1943), but he continued working.

His team included:

- **Mavis Lever** (later Batey): a 19-year-old classics student who made several critical breaks
- **Margaret Rock**: mathematician who worked on statistical analysis of the traffic
- **Keith Batey**: later married Mavis Lever

On **8 December 1941**, the team achieved the first complete break of the Abwehr Enigma settings.

## Operational Consequence — Double Cross

With the Abwehr Enigma broken, MI5 could read the communications of German agents operating in Britain. This revealed that **every single German agent in Britain** had been caught — either turned as
a double agent or imprisoned. The **XX (Double Cross) System** under John Masterman used these turned agents to feed false intelligence to the Abwehr throughout the war.

The Double Cross System's most significant achievement was **Operation Fortitude** (1944): convincing the Germans that the D-Day landings would target Pas-de-Calais rather than Normandy — with the
result that Hitler held back the Panzer reserve for six weeks after the actual landing.

The entire Double Cross operation depended on being able to read the Germans' communications with their agents — which depended on Knox's break of 8 December 1941.
