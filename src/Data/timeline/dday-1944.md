## Operation Fortitude

**Operation Fortitude** was the largest strategic deception in military history. Its goal: convince the German high command that the Allied invasion would land at **Pas-de-Calais** — the closest
  point to England — rather than Normandy.

The deception had two strands:

- **Fortitude North**: a fake threat to Norway (to pin German forces there)
- **Fortitude South**: the main deception — a fake army group (*FUSAG — First United States Army Group*) supposedly commanded by General George Patton, concentrating in East Anglia, pointing at
  Pas-de-Calais

FUSAG was entirely fictional. It consisted of false radio traffic, fake inflatable tanks, fictitious units, and — most critically — the reports of double agents run by the XX System, including:

- **GARBO** (Juan Pujol García) — a Spanish double agent
- **BRUTUS** (Roman Czerniawski) — a Polish agent
- **TRICYCLE** (Duško Popov) — a Yugoslav playboy

## Enigma Confirmation

Bletchley Park read the Abwehr's assessments of all these agents. The decrypts confirmed that the Germans believed FUSAG was real, that Patton was its commander, and that Pas-de-Calais was the
intended landing site.

GARBO sent a message to his Abwehr handler **14 hours before the Normandy landing** warning that "the attack is imminent" but that it was *not* the main attack — the main landing would follow at
Pas-de-Calais. (This message was suppressed until after the landing had begun.) The Abwehr passed GARBO's assessment to Hitler's headquarters.

## The Six-Week Delay

Even after Allied forces landed in Normandy on 6 June 1944, Hitler refused to release the **15th Army** and the Panzer reserve from Pas-de-Calais. He believed — based on GARBO's continued reporting —
that Normandy was a feint and the real blow was still coming. This belief was sustained for **six weeks** after D-Day.

Bletchley's Enigma decrypts of German high-command signals confirmed the deception was holding day by day.

## Historical Assessment

Hinsley's official history estimates that without Fortitude's success, the D-Day landings might have been repulsed or the beachhead might have been contained. The six-week withholding of the German
armoured reserve was the decisive operational difference between the Normandy landing succeeding and failing.

Eisenhower called the work of Bletchley Park and the XX System "a decisive factor in the success of Overlord."
