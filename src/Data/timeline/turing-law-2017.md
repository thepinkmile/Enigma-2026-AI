## The Law

Section 164 of the **Policing and Crime Act 2017** — informally known as the **Alan Turing Law** — amended the **Protection of Freedoms Act 2012** to provide:

1. **Posthumous pardons**: All men convicted under Section 11 of the Criminal Law Amendment Act 1885 or similar provisions for consensual same-sex conduct that is now legal received an automatic
  posthumous pardon — approximately **49,000 men**, including Turing
2. **Living individuals — disregard mechanism**: Living men with such convictions on their record could apply for a formal "disregard" — having the conviction legally set aside so it would not appear
  on criminal record checks

## Why the Law Went Beyond the Pardon

The 2013 Royal Pardon had covered only Turing. Critics — including Lord Sharkey, who had campaigned for the 2012 Lords Bill — argued that it was morally inconsistent to pardon the most famous victim
while the 49,000 others remained legally convicted.

The 2017 law addressed this. As Lord Sharkey stated during the passage of the bill:

> *"It is simply wrong that the thousands of other men convicted of the same offences as Turing have not received the same recognition."*

## Scope and Limitations

The law covers convictions for consensual same-sex conduct between adults that is no longer an offence. It does **not** cover:

- Convictions involving minors
- Convictions for public indecency
- Conduct that remains illegal today

The disregard mechanism for living people requires an application to the Home Office. As of 2023, approximately 700 applications had been received.

## Named After Turing

The informal name "Alan Turing Law" reflects Turing's position as the most prominent victim of these laws — and the direct line from the 2009 apology through the 2013 pardon to this broader statute.
His name has become synonymous with the campaign for recognition of the injustice suffered by gay men under mid-20th century British law.
