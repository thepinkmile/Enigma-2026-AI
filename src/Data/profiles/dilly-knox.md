## Biography

Alfred Dillwyn Knox was born on 23 July 1884 in Oxford, the son of the Bishop of Manchester. He won a scholarship to Eton and then to King's College, Cambridge (BA Classics 1907), where he became a
Fellow in 1909, specialising in papyrology — the study of ancient manuscripts. He was considered one of the finest classical scholars of his generation and was completing a major edition of Herodas's
*Mimes* when WWI interrupted his academic career.

## Room 40 and WWI

Knox joined the Admiralty's **Room 40** codebreaking section in 1914 and rapidly proved an exceptional cryptanalyst. He was instrumental in breaking the German naval flag codes and contributed to the
analysis of the **Zimmermann Telegram** (1917), the interception that brought the United States into WWI. He remained with what became GC&CS after the war.

## Breaking Enigma — Commercial and Italian Naval

In 1927, GC&CS purchased a commercial **Enigma D**. Knox was the first to attempt to understand its wiring and wrote an analysis noting the machine's vulnerabilities — particularly the "no letter
encrypts to itself" reflector property. However, the German military's addition of a plugboard made his analysis insufficient for breaking military traffic.

By the 1930s Knox was working on the Italian naval Enigma — a commercial variant without a plugboard. During the **Spanish Civil War** (1936–1939), German Condor Legion used a commercial Enigma
variant, and Knox's team broke some traffic. The Italian Navy used a similar no-plugboard Enigma.

### Battle of Cape Matapan (March 1941)

Working with his team at **Elmers School** (an annexe to Bletchley Park, in Bletchley town), Knox directed **Mavis Lever** and **Margaret Rock** on Italian Naval Enigma. In March 1941, Lever
recovered the Italian machine settings by exploiting an operator who had tested the machine by pressing the same key repeatedly. The cribs *PERX* → *PERSONALE* confirmed the machine type and enabled
daily setting recovery.

The resulting intelligence — a detailed signal about Italian fleet movements signed *SUPERMARINA* — allowed Admiral Cunningham to ambush the Italian fleet at **Cape Matapan** (28 March 1941), sinking
three heavy cruisers and two destroyers. It was Italy's greatest naval defeat. Cunningham visited Bletchley personally to thank Knox and his team. Knox wrote a poem: *"When Cunningham won at Matapan
/ By the grace of God and Mavis."*

### Abwehr Enigma — Broken 8 December 1941

The Abwehr used a different Enigma variant — the **Enigma G** — with no plugboard, a stepping reflector, and rotors that moved irregularly. Knox and his team (including Mavis Lever and Margaret Rock)
broke it on **8 December 1941** — the day after Pearl Harbor. In December 1941, Lever broke a critical **Belgrade–Berlin Abwehr message**, enabling recovery of rotor wiring.

This break was transformative. Reading Abwehr traffic enabled the **Double-Cross System** to verify that turned German agents were trusted by Berlin. The ISK (Illicit Services Knox) section produced
**140,800 decrypts** by war's end. Knox's Abwehr break directly enabled Operation Fortitude and D-Day's success — but Knox did not live to see it.

## Death

Knox was diagnosed with **lymphoma** in 1941. He continued working from his bed at Courns Wood, Hughenden, Buckinghamshire. He died on **27 February 1943**, aged 58, having broken the Abwehr Enigma
from his deathbed. Mavis Lever later wrote: *"He was like a father to us all. We would have done anything for him."* She published his biography, *Dilly: The Man Who Broke Enigmas* (2009), 66 years
later.
