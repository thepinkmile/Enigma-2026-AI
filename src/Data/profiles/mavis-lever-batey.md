## Biography

Mavis Lever was born on 5 May 1921 in Dulwich, south London. She won a scholarship to University College London, where she was studying German literature when WWII interrupted her studies in 1939.
Recruited to GC&CS at Bletchley Park, she was aged just 18 when she joined **Dilly Knox's section** at Elmers School — a small house in Bletchley used by Knox's team rather than the main Park.

## Breaking Italian Naval Enigma

Knox's section worked on the Italian Navy's commercial-variant Enigma (no plugboard). Lever was assigned to this task alongside **Margaret Rock**. In early 1941, she was trying to break an Italian
signal. Following Knox's method of "rodding" (a manual crib technique exploiting the reflector's self-encoding constraint), she tried the crib *PERX* (for Italian *per*, "for").

Going beyond the four letters she had been assigned, she kept going and recovered *PERSONALE* ("personal") — the full Italian word. This confirmed the machine type and yielded the settings.

The critical breakthrough came when Lever noticed that an Italian operator had clearly **tested the machine by pressing the same key repeatedly** — the encrypted sequence betrayed the all-same-letter
input. The resulting Italian signals, including one signed *SUPERMARINA* reading **"Today 25 March 1941 is the day minus three"**, gave the Royal Navy advance warning of a major Italian fleet
operation.

### Battle of Cape Matapan (28 March 1941)

Admiral Cunningham's Mediterranean Fleet, forewarned by Lever's decrypts, ambushed the Italian fleet. Three heavy cruisers (*Zara*, *Pola*, *Fiume*) and two destroyers were sunk. 2,300 Italian
sailors were killed; 1,015 captured. Allied losses: 3 killed and 1 aircraft. Cunningham visited Bletchley personally to thank Knox's team. Knox dedicated a poem to her: *"When Cunningham won at
Matapan / By the grace of God and Mavis."*

Mavis Lever was **19 years old** when she achieved this.

## Breaking Abwehr Enigma

In December 1941, Lever was working on the Abwehr Enigma (Enigma G — no plugboard, moveable reflector). She broke a critical **Belgrade–Berlin Abwehr signal**, recovering rotor wiring that Knox's
team had been seeking. This enabled the full break of the Abwehr Enigma on **8 December 1941** (Knox's last great achievement). Reading Abwehr traffic was the foundation of the **Double-Cross
System** and ultimately the D-Day deception.

## Later Life

After the war, Mavis married **Keith Batey** (a fellow Bletchley mathematician) and took his name. She became a distinguished **garden historian**, publishing multiple books on historic British
gardens, and worked extensively with the National Trust. She was awarded the **MBE** and received honorary doctorates.

In 2009 she published ***Dilly: The Man Who Broke Enigmas*** — the biography of Dilly Knox, her mentor. It remains the primary published source on Knox's cryptanalytic work.

She died on **12 November 2013**, aged 92, in Oxford.
