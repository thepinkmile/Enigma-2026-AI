## Biography

Harold Hall Keen was born in 1894 and spent virtually his entire professional life at the **British Tabulating Machine Company (BTM)**, the British licencee of Hollerith punched-card equipment. He
joined BTM in **1912** — before the First World War — was appointed head of the Experimental Department in **1923**, and rose to Chief Engineer by the 1930s. Over his career he accumulated
**more than 60 patents** in electromechanical computing and tabulating machinery.

Keen was known throughout BTM simply as **"Doc"** — a nickname earned because he habitually carried his tools in a doctor's black bag rather than in a toolbox.

## The Bombe Contract

In early 1939, Alan Turing and GC&CS approached BTM to manufacture a new electromechanical codebreaking device based on Turing's theoretical design. The contract was classified under the
codename **CANTAB** — a reference to Cambridge, where Turing had developed the theoretical framework. The total budget was approximately **£100,000**.

Keen was assigned as BTM's lead engineer on the project. He translated Turing's abstract logic into a working engineering design, solving major practical challenges:

- The drum assemblies needed to spin to exactly the right position and stop with mechanical precision; Keen's solution used a relay-based stopping mechanism that could arrest a drum mid-spin.
- The machine needed to test all 17,576 possible rotor starting positions in sequence and signal a stop reliably whenever a contradiction was absent — without jamming.
- The wiring of each drum had to perfectly simulate an Enigma rotor's scrambled internal connections and reverse-direction behaviour.

## Victory and Agnus Dei

The first Bombe, named **Victory**, was installed at Bletchley Park on **18 March 1940** (sources differ: Hinsley's official history gives 18 March; the Jones Report HW3/146 in the National
Archives gives 14 March 1940). It became operational shortly thereafter. Gordon Welchman's **Diagonal Board** enhancement — which dramatically reduced false stops — was incorporated into the
second Bombe, named **Agnus Dei**, which came online in **August 1940**.

## The High-Speed Bombes

From **March 1943**, BTM produced an improved design of Bombe with faster drum rotation, known informally as the **"Keen-type"** machine — named directly after their designer. These machines
could test rotor positions significantly faster than the original Victory design, greatly increasing the throughput of the Bletchley operation.

## Scale of Production

By the end of the war, BTM had manufactured **211 Bombe machines** under Keen's engineering direction, according to the Jones Report (HW3/146) at the National Archives, Kew. These were
distributed to Bletchley Park and several outstations including Wavendon, Adstock, and Gayhurst.

## Secrecy and Recognition

The Ultra programme remained classified under the Official Secrets Act until **1974** — the year after Keen died. He was awarded the **OBE** for his wartime engineering work (the exact date of
the award is not publicly documented), but he could never publicly explain what he had done. He died in **1973**, almost certainly without knowing that the Bombe's role in the war would ever
be publicly acknowledged.

His son, John Keen, later wrote the definitive biographical account: *Harold 'Doc' Keen and the Bletchley Park BOMBE*, M. & M. Baldwin, 2012 (ISBN 978-0-947712-48-8).
