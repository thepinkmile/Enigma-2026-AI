## Biography

Hans-Thilo Schmidt was born in Berlin on 13 May 1888, the son of a philosophy professor. His elder brother Rudolf became a highly decorated Wehrmacht general (General der Panzertruppe). This contrast
in their fortunes — Rudolf's distinguished military career against Hans-Thilo's marginal civilian role — was a source of bitterness that contributed to his decision to commit espionage.

After WWI, Schmidt worked in various commercial and government roles. In the late 1920s he obtained a position in the **Cipher Centre** of the German Foreign Office (*Chiffrierstelle*) in Berlin —
the office responsible for distributing Enigma key materials to German military and diplomatic units.

## Espionage — "Asché"

In late 1931, Schmidt made contact with French military intelligence (the **Deuxième Bureau**) and offered to sell classified documents. He was given the codename **"Asché"** (German pronunciation of
the French word *H.E.* — initials) by Gustave Bertrand, his French handler.

Between **1931 and 1941**, Schmidt met with Bertrand approximately 19 times — in Paris, Brussels, and elsewhere in Europe — selling:

- **Enigma operating instructions** (September 1931 meeting in Verviers, Belgium) — procedures for setting up and operating the machine
- **Daily key settings** for specific months — including plugboard connections, rotor selections, and ring settings
- **Cipher circuit diagrams** and spare equipment documentation

### Why His Espionage Mattered

Schmidt's materials did not directly reveal the Enigma rotor wiring — that required Rejewski's mathematics. However, they provided:

1. **Confirmation of the machine's operating procedure**, specifically the double-encipherment indicator method used before September 1938
2. **Daily key settings for specific dates** — values that Rejewski could plug into his permutation equations as known quantities, making the system of equations solvable

David Kahn (historian): *"No single spy contributed more to Allied victory in the Second World War than Hans-Thilo Schmidt."*

Without Schmidt, Rejewski's equations would have had too many unknowns. With Schmidt's key settings as initial values, Rejewski could solve for the rotor wirings. The mathematical break of December
1932 — seven years before WWII — was the direct result.

## Arrest and Death

The Germans suspected a leak for years but could not identify its source. Schmidt was eventually **arrested by the Gestapo in April 1943**, reportedly betrayed by a French intelligence officer who
had been captured. He was interrogated, tried by court-martial, and **died in German custody on 19 September 1943** — the official cause given as suicide, though the circumstances remain disputed.

His brother General Rudolf Schmidt, despite being a senior Wehrmacht commander, could not save him.
