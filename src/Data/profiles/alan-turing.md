## Biography

Alan Mathison Turing was born on 23 June 1912 at Warrington Lodge, Maida Vale, London, to Julius Mathison Turing (Indian Civil Service) and Ethel Sara Stoney. He was educated at Sherborne School,
Dorset, then won a scholarship to **King's College, Cambridge** (BA 1934, first class, mathematics). His 1936 paper *"On Computable Numbers, with an Application to the Entscheidungsproblem"*
(Proceedings of the London Mathematical Society) established the theoretical foundation for the modern computer — introducing the concept of the **Universal Turing Machine**.

In 1938 he completed a PhD at Princeton University under Alonzo Church, and returned to England just in time to be recruited by GC&CS before the outbreak of war.

## Wartime Codebreaking

Turing reported to **Bletchley Park on 4 September 1939** — the day after Britain declared war on Germany. He was assigned to **Hut 8**, the section tasked with breaking German naval Enigma.

### The Bombe

Turing's principal contribution was designing the **Bombe** — an electromechanical device for finding the daily Enigma settings. He adapted the concept from Marian Rejewski's earlier Polish *Bomba*
(1938), extending it to work against the more sophisticated plugboard Enigma. His key innovation was the **"contradictions" method**: rather than testing all possible settings directly, the Bombe
used known or guessed plaintext (*cribs*) to eliminate impossible settings.

The first Bombe, named *"Victory"*, became operational at Bletchley in **March 1940**. **Gordon Welchman** then proposed the **Diagonal Board** improvement (1940) — dramatically reducing false stops
and making the Bombe far more practical. By war's end, over 200 Bombes were operational.

### Naval Enigma (Hut 8, 1940–1942)

Turing developed **Banburismus** — a statistical technique using Hollerith punched cards and Banbury sheets to find depth (two messages in the same key) in naval traffic, allowing deduction of rotor
positions without a crib. This technique, combined with the capture of naval Enigma materials from weather ships and U-110 (May 1941), enabled continuous reading of U-boat Home Waters traffic from
June 1941.

Turing headed Hut 8 until late 1942, when he was succeeded by **Hugh Alexander**.

### Visit to Bell Labs (1942–1943)

In November 1942, Turing was sent to the USA to liaise with the US Navy and Bell Labs on advanced cryptanalytic techniques and to work on **SIGSALY** — a speech encipherment system. He met **Claude
Shannon** at Bell Labs, where they discussed information theory (Shannon's paper was classified at the time). On his return, he helped design the **Delilah** voice-scrambling device.

### Post-War

- 1945: Awarded OBE for wartime service (citation deliberately vague)
- 1945–1948: Designed the **Automatic Computing Engine (ACE)** at the National Physical Laboratory, Teddington
- 1948: Joined Max Newman's Computing Machine Laboratory, University of Manchester
- 1950: Published *"Computing Machinery and Intelligence"* (Mind, Vol. 59, No. 236) — proposing what became known as the **Turing Test**
- 1951: Elected Fellow of the Royal Society (FRS)
- 1952: Convicted of "gross indecency" under Section 11, Criminal Law Amendment Act 1885 for his relationship with Arnold Murray; sentenced to 12 months' probation + compulsory hormone injections
  (stilboestrol)
- **7 June 1954**: Found dead at 43 Adlington Road, Wilmslow. Inquest verdict: suicide by cyanide poisoning. A half-eaten apple lay beside him. He was 41.
