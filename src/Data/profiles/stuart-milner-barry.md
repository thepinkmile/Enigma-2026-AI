## Biography

Philip Stuart Milner-Barry was born in 1906 in Hendon, London. He was educated at Cheltenham College and Trinity College, Cambridge, where he read Classics and then Moral Sciences (Philosophy). At
Cambridge he became a strong chess player and formed a lasting friendship with Hugh Alexander, with whom he would later work at Bletchley Park.

After Cambridge he worked as a stockbroker in the City of London, continuing to play chess at the highest level — he represented England in three Chess Olympiads alongside Alexander and was a British
Championship contender throughout the 1930s.

## Bletchley Park — Hut 6

Milner-Barry arrived at Bletchley Park in January 1940, recruited through his chess connections (like Alexander). He was assigned to **Hut 6** — the section responsible for breaking German Army and
Luftwaffe Enigma traffic (as distinct from Hut 8, which handled Naval Enigma).

When Gordon Welchman was moved to administrative duties, Milner-Barry became **head of Hut 6**. Under his leadership the section broke and exploited:

- **Red** (Luftwaffe main key — broken from 22 May 1940, the first major wartime Enigma break)
- **Chaffinch** (Germany–North Africa Panzer communications — critical during the Western Desert Campaign)
- Multiple Army keys supporting planning for D-Day and the Northwest Europe campaign

## The "Geese" Letter and Churchill's Visit

On 21 October 1941, Milner-Barry was personally responsible for **hand-delivering to Downing Street** the famous letter from Turing, Welchman, Alexander, and himself to Winston Churchill — the letter
that complained about resource bottlenecks and requested more Bombe machines and WRNS operators.

The reason Milner-Barry carried the letter himself was pragmatic: Welchman had written it, but access to Churchill was uncertain. Milner-Barry simply turned up at 10 Downing Street, explained it was
a matter of the highest national importance, and was eventually admitted to hand the letter to Churchill's secretary.

Churchill's response — "Action This Day" — resulted in an immediate release of resources. The Bombe programme accelerated, and WRNS recruitment for Bletchley increased substantially. It was arguably
the most consequential personal errand of the war.

## Post-War Career

After the war Milner-Barry joined **HM Treasury** as a senior civil servant. He rose to be an Under-Secretary and was knighted (CB) in 1962. He did not return to chess professionally but continued to
play and write about the game.

Like most Bletchley veterans, he maintained absolute secrecy about his wartime work until the Ultra declassification in 1974. In his later years he wrote and spoke publicly about Hut 6 and advocated
strongly for recognition of the Polish contribution to breaking Enigma, which he felt had been systematically under-acknowledged.

He died in 1995, having lived long enough to see the full story of Bletchley Park enter the public record.
