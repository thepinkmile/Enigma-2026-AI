## Biography

Hugo Alexander Koch was a Dutch inventor working in the Netherlands in the early twentieth century. His background and early life are not well documented, but he was active as an inventor in the
fields of mechanical and electromechanical apparatus in the 1910s.

## Dutch Patent NL10700 (7 October 1919)

On **7 October 1919** — approximately twenty months after Arthur Scherbius had filed his first German patent (23 February 1918) — Hugo Koch filed Dutch patent **NL10700** for a *geheimschrijfmachine*
(secret writing machine) based on rotating electrical commutators (rotors).

Koch's machine was conceptually similar to Scherbius's design: a sequence of wired rotor discs that substituted letters electrically, with the rotors stepping to change the substitution with each
character encoded. Koch was apparently unaware of Scherbius's prior patent when he filed.

The patent was published and registered. Koch made limited commercial efforts to sell the machine independently but lacked Scherbius's business infrastructure.

## Sale of Patent to Scherbius

In **1927**, Hugo Koch sold his patent rights to **Arthur Scherbius and ChiMaAG**. This purchase was commercially significant: it consolidated rotor machine intellectual property into a single entity
and removed a potential competitor. After the sale, ChiMaAG (and later Heimsoeth und Rinke) could manufacture and sell Enigma machines without the risk of competing patent claims in the Netherlands
or internationally.

The precise sale price is not recorded in public sources, but the transfer is documented in patent records.

## Historical Significance

Koch's independent invention demonstrates that the rotor machine concept was in the air among European cryptographic inventors immediately after WWI. Within a two-year window (1918–1920), at least
four inventors in four countries independently arrived at similar designs:

- **Arthur Scherbius** (Germany) — February 1918
- **Hugo Koch** (Netherlands) — October 1919
- **Edward Hebern** (USA) — patent 1921
- **Arvid Damm** (Sweden) — patent 1919

This convergence reflects the general recognition after WWI that the telegraph and radio had created urgent demand for mechanical encryption of high-volume military communications.

Koch's specific contribution — the Dutch patent — gave Scherbius's commercial enterprise a cleaner legal foundation in international markets and may have contributed to early military sales.
